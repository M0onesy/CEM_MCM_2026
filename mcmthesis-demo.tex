\title{A Dual-Track Decision System for Professional Sports Management: Integrating Performance and Finance}

\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Professional sports organizations must constantly reconcile two competing objectives: winning games and remaining financially viable. In the evolving WNBA environment—characterized by salary caps, roster constraints, and rapid revenue growth—the tension between performance and profitability becomes especially pronounced. Using the Indiana Fever as a representative case, we develop a structured decision framework that evaluates how superstar-driven commercial expansion can be translated into long-term competitive and financial stability.

We first construct a \textbf{Dual-Dimension Player Valuation Model} that separates total player value into \textbf{performance contribution} and \textbf{commercial impact}. On-court value is quantified through a WAR-based marginal win approach, while off-court value is attributed using observable revenue proxies and demand indicators. This decomposition highlights the degree of value concentration in high-visibility players and provides a principled basis for balancing salary allocation with brand externalities.

Building on this valuation layer, we design a \textbf{Financial Forecasting and Revenue Optimization System} linking roster composition to ticket sales, sponsorship income, broadcast exposure, and merchandise revenue. A \textbf{Dynamic Ticket Pricing Model} adjusts game-level prices based on opponent tier, demand elasticity, and arena capacity constraints. These revenue projections feed directly into a \textbf{Mixed-Integer Roster Optimization Framework}, which selects feasible lineups under salary cap and positional restrictions while maximizing a profit-weighted competitive objective.

To incorporate uncertainty, we introduce a \textbf{State-Transition Decision Engine} that updates strategic parameters when predefined thresholds—such as injury events, performance deviations, or league structural changes—are triggered. Player health risk is modeled probabilistically, and scenario evaluation ensures robustness under playoff variability and expansion shocks. In addition, a \textbf{Gravity-Based Geographic Competition Model} quantifies regional market overlap and evaluates expansion-induced revenue risk.

Under current assumptions, disciplined load management, targeted defensive investment, calibrated pricing adjustments, and proactive sponsorship stabilization jointly improve projected operating performance while maintaining playoff-level competitiveness. Rather than optimizing wins or revenue in isolation, the framework offers a quantitative method for evaluating strategic trade-offs in regulated professional sports environments.

Overall, this study provides an integrated yet operational modeling approach that connects player valuation, financial projection, roster construction, and dynamic adjustment into a unified decision-support structure.

Sports Analytics; WNBA; Dynamic Decision Making; Shapley Value; Player Valuation; Mixed-Integer Programming; Monte Carlo Simulation; Markov Decision Process
\end{keywords}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Background}
Professional sports represent a unique intersection of athletic competition and business enterprise. The central tension---whether players should be valued for their contribution to wins or to owner profit---demands a rigorous modeling framework that integrates both dimensions \cite{fort2004}. The Women's National Basketball Association (WNBA) provides a particularly compelling context: record viewership in 2024 (averaging 1.18 million viewers per game, up 170\% from 2023 \cite{espn2024wnba}), rising franchise valuations, and ongoing CBA negotiations create a strategic environment where data-driven decision-making can yield outsized returns.

\subsection{Team Selection: Indiana Fever}
We select the Indiana Fever as our primary case study for three compelling reasons:

\textbf{First,} the team exemplifies the performance-versus-profit tension at its most acute. Rookie Caitlin Clark generated commercial value exceeding her statistical contribution by a factor of 11.3:1, creating a unique optimization landscape where traditional basketball metrics undervalue the player's true organizational worth \cite{sportico2024fever}.

\textbf{Second,} with an Indianapolis MSA population of 2.1 million \cite{census2024}, the Fever represents a small-market franchise that suddenly commands national attention, creating strategic challenges around market defense, pricing optimization, and sustainable growth that differ fundamentally from large-market teams.

\textbf{Third,} the team's 2024 transformation---from a losing record to playoff contention accompanied by approximately 300\% revenue growth \cite{forbes2024wnba}---provides rich, recent data for model calibration and validation.

\section{Dynamic Decision-Making Model}

This section addresses Task 1: designing a dynamic decision-making model to help ownership and management adjust leverage in response to changing team performance and economic conditions.

\subsection{Dual-Track Management Framework}

We conceptualize team management as two parallel optimization tracks \cite{dietl2011}: 

\textbf{Track A (Business Operations)} focuses on revenue maximization, cost management, capital structure, and market positioning; 

\textbf{Track B (Basketball Operations)} concentrates on win maximization, roster construction, player development, and game strategy. 

These tracks are linked through an integrated objective function:

\begin{equation}
\text{maximize: } V = \alpha \cdot \text{Profit\_Index} + (1-\alpha) \cdot \text{Win\_Index}
\end{equation}
where both indices are normalized to $[0,1]$ scales, and $\alpha \in [0,1]$ represents the relative weight on financial versus competitive objectives.

\subsubsection{AHP-Based Weight Calibration}
We employ the \textbf{Analytic Hierarchy Process} (AHP) \cite{saaty1980} to derive $\alpha$. A pairwise comparison matrix across four strategic criteria (Revenue Growth, Franchise Valuation, CBA Uncertainty, Competitive Window) yields priority weights via the principal eigenvector: Revenue (0.28), Valuation (0.17), CBA Risk (0.39), Competitive (0.16). The consistency ratio $CR = 0.042 < 0.10$ confirms acceptable consistency. The three financially-oriented criteria sum to 0.84, producing $\alpha = 0.55$, reflecting strategic priority of financial stability while maintaining competitive integrity.

\subsection{Model A: Dual-Dimension Player Value Assessment}
Central to our framework is a dual-dimension player valuation model:
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{total}} = \beta \cdot V_{\text{performance}} + (1-\beta) \cdot V_{\text{commercial}}
\end{equation}
where $\beta = 0.4$ (calibrated via AHP for commercially-driven franchises like the Fever).

\textbf{Performance Value} is derived from Wins Above Replacement (WAR) \cite{kubatko2007}:
\begin{equation}
\text{WAR} = \frac{(\text{BPM} - \text{BPM}_{\text{replacement}}) \times \text{Minutes\_Played}}{48\,(\text{min/game}) \times 40\,(\text{games})}, \quad V_{\text{performance}} = \text{WAR} \times \text{VPW}
\end{equation}
where $\text{BPM}_{\text{replacement}} = -2.0$ and VPW $\approx$ \$300,000 (see Appendix A for calibration details).

\textbf{Commercial Value via Shapley Attribution:} We formalize commercial value decomposition using the Shapley value from cooperative game theory \cite{shapley1953}. For coalition game $(N, v)$ where $N$ is the roster and $v(S)$ is incremental revenue from subset $S$:
\begin{equation}
\phi_i(v) = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|!\,(|N|-|S|-1)!}{|N|!} \left[ v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S) \right]
\end{equation}

We approximate using a market influence index $M_i = 0.35 \cdot \tilde{S}_i + 0.30 \cdot \tilde{J}_i + 0.35 \cdot \tilde{T}_i$ (normalized social media reach, jersey sales rank, TV rating impact), yielding:
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{commercial},i} = \frac{M_i}{\sum_j M_j} \times (R_{\text{total}} - R_{\text{baseline}})
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{baseline}} = \$16.5$M represents the Fever's pre-Clark revenue level (2023 baseline \cite{forbes2024wnba}).

A critical modeling choice is the method for attributing commercial revenue to individual players. We compare four candidate approaches:

\textbf{Method 1: Equal Split} divides incremental revenue equally among roster players ($\$47.3\text{M} / 12 = \$3.94\text{M}$). This naive approach drastically undervalues superstars and overvalues bench players, violating the obvious reality that Clark drives the majority of commercial interest.

\textbf{Method 2: Proportional (Single Metric)} allocates revenue proportional to a single proxy (e.g., social media followers). Clark has 5.2M Instagram followers versus $\sim$650K total for all other Fever players combined, yielding 88.9\% attribution. This \textit{overstates} her value by ignoring synergies---some fans follow the \textit{team} because of Clark but also engage with other players.

\textbf{Method 3: Linear Weighted Index} uses $V_i = \Delta R \times (w_1 \tilde{S}_i + w_2 \tilde{J}_i + w_3 \tilde{T}_i)$, where weights sum to 1. This improves on single-metric approaches but still assumes \textit{additive separability}---it cannot capture the fact that Clark's presence amplifies the commercial value of teammates (e.g., Boston's jersey sales increased 340\% when paired with Clark).

\textbf{Method 4: Shapley Value} is the unique allocation satisfying four axioms: \textit{efficiency} (values sum to total surplus), \textit{symmetry} (equal contributors get equal value), \textit{null player} (non-contributors get zero), and \textit{additivity} (consistent across decomposable games) \cite{shapley1953}. Critically, it captures \textit{interaction effects}: if Clark's presence increases Boston's marginal revenue contribution, this synergy is properly split between them.

\textbf{Empirical Validation:} We test each method's predictive accuracy using a leave-one-out approach on 2024 WNBA data. For each team, we predict the revenue change from hypothetically removing each player and compare to actual mid-season injury impacts (n=23 significant injuries across the league):

\subsection{Model D: State-Transition Decision Engine}
We formalize the dynamic decision problem as a finite-horizon Markov Decision Process (MDP) \cite{puterman1994}, defined by tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, T)$:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{State space} $\mathcal{S}$: Each state $s_t = (s_t^B, s_t^K, s_t^P, s_t^E)$ encodes business state (revenue trajectory: \textit{surge/stable/decline}), basketball state (record tier: \textit{contender/middle/lottery}), player state (star health: \textit{healthy/minor/major}), and external state (CBA/expansion: \textit{stable/uncertain/changed}). This yields $3^4 = 81$ discrete states.
\item \textbf{Action space} $\mathcal{A}$: \{\textit{aggressive invest, hold steady, retrench, trade for win-now, develop youth, activate insurance, adjust pricing, lock sponsors}\}.
\item \textbf{Transition probabilities} $P(s_{t+1} | s_t, a_t)$: Estimated from 2019--2024 WNBA team histories (see Appendix A for transition matrix).
\item \textbf{Reward function} $R(s_t, a_t) = \alpha \cdot \pi(s_t, a_t) + (1-\alpha) \cdot W(s_t, a_t)$.
\item \textbf{Horizon} $T = 40$ (game-by-game decisions, reviewed biweekly).
\end{itemize}

\end{algorithmic}

\subsection{Model E: Capital Structure Optimization}
The optimal capital structure minimizes the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) \cite{brealey2020}:
\begin{equation}
\text{WACC} = \frac{E}{V} \times r_e + \frac{D}{V} \times r_d \times (1 - T_c), 
\end{equation}

The cost of equity follows CAPM with Hamada’s leverage adjustment:
\begin{equation}
\quad r_e = r_f + \beta_L \times (r_m - r_f)
\end{equation}


where $\beta_L = \beta_U \times [1 + (1-T_c) \times D/E]$ (Hamada's leverage adjustment \cite{hamada1972}). 

Using $r_f = 4.5\%$, $r_m - r_f = 5.5\%$, $\beta_U = 0.9$ (\cite{damodaran2024}), 

the cost of debt follows $r_d = r_{\text{base}} + 0.02 \times (D/V / 0.30)^2$.

\subsection{Model C: Roster Optimization Model}
The roster optimization problem is formulated as a mixed-integer linear program (MILP) \cite{bertsimas1997}. Let $x_i \in \{0,1\}$ indicate whether player $i$ is on the roster:
\begin{equation}
\text{maximize } \sum_{i \in \mathcal{P}} V_i \times x_i \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_{i} S_i x_i \leq \$1.4\text{M}, \quad 11 \leq \sum_i x_i \leq 12
\end{equation}
with position constraints ($\geq$4 guards, $\geq$3 forwards, $\geq$2 centers) and core retention ($x_i = 1$ for Clark, Boston, Mitchell).

\textbf{Surplus Value:} $\text{Surplus}_i = V_{\text{total},i} - S_i$. Clark's surplus (\$9.66M $-$ \$0.077M $=$ \$9.58M) is by far the largest in the WNBA.

\subsection{Model F: Player Potential Projection}
The age adjustment factor captures typical WNBA career trajectory (calibrated from 301 player-seasons \cite{bbref2024}):
\begin{equation}
f(\text{age}) = \begin{cases}
0.70 + 0.10 \times (\text{age}-22) & \text{if } 22 \leq \text{age} < 25 \\
1.00 + 0.02 \times (\text{age}-25) & \text{if } 25 \leq \text{age} < 29 \\
1.08 - 0.05 \times (\text{age}-29) & \text{if } \text{age} \geq 29
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

An injury risk factor $r_{\text{injury}} = 1 - [0.05 + 0.01 \times \max(0, \text{age}-28) + 0.03 \times N_{\text{injuries}} + 0.01 \times \max(0, \text{MPG}-32)]$ further adjusts projections \cite{drakos2010}. Contract NPV discounts projected values: $\text{NPV} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} V_{\text{projected}}(t) \times f(\text{age}_t) \times r_{\text{injury}}(t) / (1+r)^t$ where $r = 8\%$.

\section{League Expansion Impact Analysis}

\subsection{Model G: Geographic Competition Model}
We model market competition using a gravity-based overlap function \cite{huff1964}. Competitive intensity decreases with geographic distance following exponential decay \cite{humphreys2008}:
\begin{equation}
\text{Overlap}_{ij} = \frac{\min(\text{Pop}_i, \text{Pop}_j)}{\text{Pop}_i} \times e^{-\gamma \times d_{ij}}
\end{equation}
where $\text{Pop}_i, \text{Pop}_j$ are MSA populations \cite{census2024}, $d_{ij}$ is Haversine distance (km), and $\gamma = 0.003$ km$^{-1}$ (see Appendix A for calibration via WNBA attendance data).

Revenue impact combines direct market cannibalization with indirect effects:
\begin{equation}
\Delta R_i = -R_{\text{base}} \times \text{Overlap}_{ij} \times \alpha_{\text{impact}} + R_{\text{national}} \times \delta_{\text{expansion}}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{\text{impact}} = 0.15$ (15\% revenue loss at 100\% overlap) and $\delta_{\text{expansion}}$ captures incremental national revenue sharing ($+$\$0.3--0.5M per expansion team).

\subsection*{A.1 Value Per Win (VPW) Estimation}

We estimate VPW using a two-stage approach combining attendance-wins regression and revenue decomposition.

\textbf{Stage 1: Attendance-Wins Relationship.} Using 2019--2024 WNBA game-level data (n = 1,840 team-games), we regress home attendance on team win percentage and control variables:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attendance}_g = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{WinPct}_t + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Weekend}_g + \beta_3 \cdot \text{Opponent}_g + \epsilon_g
\end{equation}

\centering
\caption{Attendance Regression Results}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Variable} & \textbf{Coefficient} & \textbf{Std.\ Error} & \textbf{$p$-value} \\
\midrule
Intercept & 4,521 & 312 & $<$0.001 \\
Win Percentage & 8,245 & 892 & $<$0.001 \\
Weekend Dummy & 1,872 & 156 & $<$0.001 \\
Opponent Tier (S/A) & 2,134 & 203 & $<$0.001 \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{2}{l}{$R^2 = 0.423$} & \multicolumn{2}{l}{$n = 1{,}840$} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The coefficient $\beta_1 = 8{,}245$ implies that a 10-percentage-point increase in win rate (approximately 4 wins in a 40-game season) increases average attendance by 825 fans per game.

\textbf{Stage 2: Revenue per Fan.} Based on Fever 2024 financials \cite{forbes2024wnba}, we estimate per-fan revenue:
\begin{itemize}
\item Ticket revenue: \$45 average ticket price
\item Concessions/parking: \$18 per attendee
\item Merchandise uplift: \$8 per attendee (allocated)
\item \textbf{Total per-fan revenue: \$71}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{VPW Calculation:}
\begin{align}
\text{Marginal attendance per win} &= \frac{8{,}245}{40} \times 20 \text{ home games} = 4{,}123 \text{ fans/season} \\
\text{Direct revenue per win} &= 4{,}123 \times \$71 = \$292{,}733 \\
\text{Sponsorship/TV uplift} &\approx 2.5\% \text{ of direct} = \$7{,}318 \\
\textbf{VPW} &\approx \$300{,}000 \quad \text{(95\% CI: [\$220K, \$380K])}
\end{align}

\subsection*{A.2 Distance Decay Parameter ($\gamma$) Estimation}

We estimate $\gamma$ using WNBA road attendance data (2019--2024), regressing visiting team fan presence on distance:
\begin{equation}
\log(\text{VisitorShare}_g) = \alpha - \gamma \cdot d_{ij} + \epsilon_g
\end{equation}
where $\text{VisitorShare}_g$ is the estimated proportion of road fans (proxied by visiting team merchandise sales at arena).

\begin{equation}
